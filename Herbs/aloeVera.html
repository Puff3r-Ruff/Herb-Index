Aloe Vera

Aloe barbadensis

Habitat: Native to arid regions of Africa; now cultivated worldwide.

Plant parts used: Inner leaf gel and bitter latex.

Constituents

Aloe Vera contains polysaccharides, plant sterols, enzymes, vitamins, minerals, and anthraquinones (in the latex). These compounds contribute to its long-standing use as a cooling, soothing, and hydrating plant.

Actions

Traditionally, Aloe Vera has been valued for its cooling, moistening, and skin-supporting qualities. The inner gel has been used to soothe the skin, while the latex was historically used in small amounts as a strong digestive stimulant.

Traditional uses

Aloe Vera has been used for thousands of years in African, Mediterranean, and Middle Eastern herbal traditions. It was commonly applied to the skin for comfort and hydration, and the inner gel was valued as a general wellness plant.

Current uses & research

Today, Aloe Vera is widely used in skincare, wellness products, and traditional herbal practice. Research continues to explore its polysaccharides and their potential roles in supporting normal skin function and hydration.

Key preparations

  • Fresh gel: Used directly from the leaf.
  • Juice: Made from the inner gel.
  • Topical gels/creams: Common in skincare.

Practical self-help uses

Aloe Vera gel is often used in traditional self-care for skin comfort and hydration. Aloe juice is included in some wellness traditions for digestive support. These uses reflect traditional herbal practice rather than medical treatment.
